Project Overview

After watching countless videos online, reading blogs and talking to Mazda MX-5 owners, I finally reached the point where I could buy one myself. I have always loved the community around the car and I was excited by the idea of modifying it to discover how an already great driver's car could become even more engaging. I wanted to experience for myself how small changes could transform the feeling of the car every time you start it, brake hard into a corner, or turn in at speed.

I did not have much experience working on cars when I started. However, my dad always did the maintenance on his own cars and the internet is full of passionate MX-5 owners who enjoy wrenching on theirs. With that in mind, I began learning, fixing things myself and gradually modifying the car to match my own preferences.

Maintenance

After a month of owning my car I scheduled major maintenance for it. I am doing the full brake system, replacing the wheel bearings and a fluid change.

Check engine light

I bought the car with a check engine light. There was a fault where the alternator feedback voltage was not sensed by the ECU. After a lot of searching I found an SMD resistor that was bad. Replaced it and the issue was gone. Never heard of a resistor going bad, but there is a first for everything I guess. While I was in here I also replaced the main connector of the ECU, because there was rust on the pins of the connector. I could see that there was a leak in the past, because there was a clear flood line visible in the footwell where the ECU sits.

Fixing rust

A common place where the MX5 rusts out is down in the quarter panel. This is the place where water drains that falls on the windshield of the car. When the car is badly maintained leaves and sand piles up in the quarter panel making it a place to stay wet for long and in the end starts rusting.

While not the prettiest option I decided to fix both quarter panels, because I liked the idea of doing some body work. I will be replacing the quarter panels in the future when a full repaint of the car is on the to-do list.

Leaking steering rack

Power steering was leaking, so I took this opportunity to clean up the complete steering rack and replace the leaking line.

First mod time!

I am replacing the stock suspension with a set of BC-racing BR coilovers and thereby lowering the car.

This upgrade gives the car a lot more grip during cornering and a more direct feel in the steering wheel. Although the downside is that the suspension is a lot stiffer, with the BC-racing coilovers it is possible to firstly adjust the damping force and secondly you can buy softer springs if you desire.

Rims & wheels

Of course a good suspension needs some grippy rubber. The old tires were all-season tires and such a car needs summer tires at least. Combined with the new tires I opted to also buy some new rims. I really wanted to go with bronze rims so I chose the JR11's 15*8 with 205/55/15 tires.

Fixing rust

Another common place where the second generation MX5 rusts is the front frame rails. On the second gen MX-5's Mazda needed to thicken the frame rails in the front in order to have better crash tests, so Mazda decided to just place another sheet of steel over the other. This way the chassis rusts from the inside out. The previous owner had this fixed in an unprofessional way, so I decided to replace this with a ready-made patch panel.

Cooling system upgrade

The cooling system on the MX-5 is pretty weak. Originally the engine used in the MX-5 was developed for a front wheel drive car. Mazda decided to move the thermostat to the front of the engine, because the rear is hard to reach since this is now against the firewall. This comes with a big consequence though; the coolant enters the engine at the water pump in the front and is pumped up into the head where the exit is directly located above the water pump. Therefore cylinder 4 has bad water flow making this cylinder overheat under high loads. So a common mod is to do a coolant reroute which moves the thermostat to the back of the head giving cylinder four much better cooling. Paired with this I installed a 40 mm aluminum radiator.

Nürburgring

With a buddy of mine I had my first experience driving my own car on a race track. Not all went smooth sailing. Before going on this trip I had done a coolant reroute. In order to make this fit I had to delete the EGR. During the drive to Germany the block-off nut came loose from the exhaust manifold, making cylinder 4 leak. Once arrived on the camping grounds we fixed this temporarily with some exhaust tape.

Torsen 2

This winter my car does not have to go to winter storage, because I have my own garage where it can wait for better weather again. This year I took this opportunity to install a Torsen 2 differential. While I was in there I also replaced the rear main seal, reinforced the diff housing, because they can snap when making more power than stock and upgraded to stiffer differential bushings.
